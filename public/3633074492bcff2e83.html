Labor & Policy

House bill would finally open H-2A to year-round dairy labor

House Agriculture Chairman Glenn "GT" Thompson is introducing the Securing Agriculture's Workforce Act, which redefines "temporary" by contract length rather than season — letting dairies, processors and livestock operations hire H-2A workers for non-seasonal, year-round jobs (up to 350 days) for the first time, while capping annual H-2A wage increases at 3.5%.

Why it matters: dairy has been effectively locked out of H-2A because the work isn't seasonal. If this advances, it reshapes the labor-cost and labor-availability math that every year-round operation lives with.

Animal Health

USDA commits $105M to 40 screwworm "Grand Challenge" projects

APHIS selected 40 of 226 applicants for about $105 million to modernize sterile-fly production, sharpen detection, and accelerate treatments against New World screwworm — the competitive program launched in January as U.S.-linked case counts climbed.

Why it matters: a northward screwworm spread is a direct threat to cattle; the speed and quality of detection and sterile-fly capacity determine how contained — or costly — an incursion becomes for the herd.

Logistics

Pennsylvania advances higher milk-truck weight limits

The Pennsylvania House Transportation Committee unanimously moved a bill raising milk-hauler weight limits to 95,000 lbs on a five-axle truck and up to 117,000 lbs on a permitted seven-axle rig, from the current 80,000-lb baseline — a compromise negotiated with the governor's administration.

Why it matters: heavier legal loads mean fewer trips and lower per-cwt hauling cost, and bring the state in line with neighbors — the kind of milk-assembly economics that move freight bills anywhere haul rates are tight.

Manure & Energy

World-first: raw dairy biogas converted to jet fuel on-site

Circularity Fuels says a six-month California pilot turned raw manure-digester biogas (~65% methane) directly into ASTM-spec sustainable aviation fuel using modular, skid-mounted reactors — at a claimed ~one-fifth the capital cost of European SAF plants now under construction. A first commercial-scale build is targeted for 2027.

Why it matters: if it scales, on-farm SAF is a potential new monetization path for digester gas beyond pipeline RNG and electricity — worth tracking for anyone weighing a digester's payback.

Processing & Co-ops

Good Culture and MMPA stand up a dedicated cottage-cheese plant

Cottage-cheese brand Good Culture partnered with farmer-owned cooperative MMPA, which acquired a Remus, Michigan facility (launched May 2026) to produce Good Culture's full line. The brand has roughly quadrupled sales in three years amid about 60% growth in the cottage-cheese category.

Why it matters: it signals where cooperative processing capital and Class-utilization demand are flowing — cultured, high-protein dairy rather than commodity cheese — which shapes whose milk gets pulled and at what premium.

Competition

Danone sues Chobani over "20g Protein" labeling

Danone filed suit in the Southern District of New York (June 15) alleging Chobani's multi-serve "20g Protein" tubs overstate protein by using a generous serving size — closer to 17.8g on a two-thirds-cup basis — to undercut Danone's Oikos Pro on shelf price.

Why it matters: a court fight over how protein is counted per serving could set labeling precedent across the fast-growing high-protein dairy category that's pulling milk into value-added uses.

Markets

Powder is the market's pressure valve — and capacity is outrunning milk

CME nonfat dry milk has slid sharply (down roughly 20¢ in a week) and the Global Dairy Trade index fell 2.8% on June 16. Daily Dairy Report's Sarina Sharp notes new cheese, butter and yogurt plants are being built faster than milk supply is growing; once they fill, dryers again absorb the overflow — pointing to more NDM output and, eventually, lower powder prices.

Why it matters: powder and Class IV weakness can drag blend prices even when cheese holds firm — a reason to watch the spread, not just the cheese block.
