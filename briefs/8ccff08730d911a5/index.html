Labor & Policy

Dairy gets a door into the H-2A visa program — with strings attached

DHS, the Department of Labor and USCIS issued guidance on June 17 clarifying that dairies can petition for H-2A temporary agricultural workers — but only where they can document a genuine temporary or seasonal need. Year-round milking roles still don't qualify, and back-to-back petitions for the same position can support a denial.

Why it matters: it's the first real federal pathway to legal seasonal labor for dairy, yet it won't plug a permanent, year-round labor gap — and aggressive or repeat filings invite scrutiny.

Source: USDA•Cornell Ag Workforce analysis

Markets

Nonfat dry milk is in free-fall — watch your Class IV check

CME spot nonfat dry milk slid from $2.1450/lb on June 3 to roughly $1.64/lb by June 19 — close to a 50-cent drop in under three weeks, including a 7.5-cent single-day fall on June 16.

Why it matters: NDM sets the skim-solids piece of the Class IV price, so a powder rout pressures every milk check with Class IV exposure and signals softening global powder demand.

Source: CME Group — Nonfat Dry Milk

Animal Health & Biosecurity

“Operation Sterile Fly”: USDA scales up its screwworm defense on the border

USDA APHIS is now dispersing roughly 100 million sterile New World screwworm flies per week, opened a dispersal facility at Moore Air Base in Edinburg, Texas in February, and broke ground on a domestic sterile-fly production plant on April 17.

Why it matters: a screwworm incursion would turn any wound, fresh calving or dehorning into a reportable infestation risk and tighten cattle-movement scrutiny — a herd-health threat worth tracking before it moves north.

Source: USDA APHIS — Stop Screwworm

Processing & Cooperatives

A co-op bets on cottage cheese: MMPA and Good Culture go live in Remus

Michigan Milk Producers Association held a ribbon-cutting on June 16 for its former-Leprino plant in Remus, Michigan and named Good Culture as its cottage-cheese partner — opening a new, branded home for member milk.

Why it matters: with fluid demand eroding, cooperatives converting member milk into branded, high-protein products is the template for defending farmgate demand and capturing margin downstream.

Source: Cheese Reporter•MMPA

Input Costs

Urea hits a 2026 low as the market eyes the Strait of Hormuz

Urea, the workhorse nitrogen fertilizer, has fallen to its lowest price this year and fuel has eased, as traders wait to see whether Middle East tensions disrupt global trade flows out of the region.

Why it matters: cheaper nitrogen and diesel trim the cost of this season's corn silage and hay — a rare input-cost tailwind running against soft milk prices, though a Hormuz disruption could reverse it quickly.

Source: Agri-Pulse

Feed & Outlook · Background

The long game: corn could shed a North-Carolina-sized chunk of acreage without new biofuel demand

An S&P Global study warns U.S. corn plantings could fall 31% by 2050 — an area roughly the size of North Carolina — if new demand-boosting markets such as biofuels aren't secured.

Why it matters: corn is the backbone of feed cost. A structural shift in corn's demand base — toward or away from ethanol and sustainable aviation fuel — is a long-run driver of what it costs to fill the bunker.

Source: Agri-Pulse
