01Sustainability · Feed

Grazing's climate edge shrinks once you count the feed

A new Cambridge-led analysis finds the climate benefit of cutting back grazing can fall by up to roughly 31% once you count emissions from the bought-in feed that replaces pasture — and the net result depends almost entirely on where that feed comes from.

Why it matters: If carbon claims, program payments, or a "graze less, feed more" plan rest on simple grazing math, it may not hold up unless your supplemental feed is genuinely low-footprint and sourced locally.

02Animal Health

Invasive tick — and the cattle disease it carries — now in 27 states

The Asian longhorned tick, which spreads Theileria orientalis Ikeda (anemia, weakness, abortions, and milk-production losses), has now been found in 27 states; Theileria is confirmed in 59 of Missouri's 114 counties, with recent cattle deaths reported in Tennessee and Nebraska.

Why it matters: The parasite mimics anaplasmosis, spreads via a self-cloning tick, and has no easy cure — worth scouting herds and incoming replacements and setting a testing plan with your vet before it reaches your area.

03Precision · Supply

A milk co-op is grading 700 farms from space

UK co-op First Milk is rolling out satellite-led risk mapping plus two years of nutrient footprinting across roughly 700 member farms to target water-quality action field by field — with early data suggesting low-footprint farms can match the output of high-footprint ones.

Why it matters: A concrete preview of where buyer sustainability rules are heading — from whole-farm averages to precise, field-level compliance that can determine whose milk gets bought and at what terms.

04Processing

Northeast processing keeps shrinking; one small restart is "a ray of hope"

A shuttered Franklin County, Vermont plant will reopen this fall as a small Baker's cheese and plant-based cream cheese operation (~20 jobs, down from ~100) — even as Dairy Farmers of America closes its St. Albans plant in August and Hood's Barre plant shut in April.

Why it matters: Thinning regional processing and balancing capacity reduces milk-buyer competition and raises the stakes on your plant's intake terms and hauling — a contraction pattern worth watching beyond the Northeast.

05Supply Chain · Capital

Ortega's property arm buys Lactalis Canada's biggest hub — and leases it back

Amancio Ortega's Pontegadea acquired Lactalis Canada's 379,000-sq-ft Oshawa, Ontario distribution hub for about US$187M in an off-market sale-leaseback, with Lactalis staying on a 24-year lease.

Why it matters: Major processors are converting fixed cold-chain assets into cash as outside real-estate capital moves into dairy logistics — a financing shift that can quietly reshape processor balance sheets and, eventually, the prices they pay and charge.

06Global Markets

Irish farmers press Brussels for paid milk-supply cuts

With farmgate prices reportedly sliding from over 50c toward below 33c/L and a projected €1.3bn hit, Ireland's ICMSA is demanding the EU immediately reopen a voluntary milk-supply-reduction scheme like the one it ran in 2016.

Why it matters: If the EU again pays farmers to produce less, it tightens global milk supply and supports the export-market prices US sellers compete against — an early, bullish signal for the world dairy complex.

07Policy · Feed

A regenerative-ag order quietly opens a biofuel market for feed grains

Alongside the June 25 regenerative-agriculture executive order, USDA unveiled a "Regenerative Feedstock Rule" linking regeneratively grown corn, soybeans, sorghum and canola to the biofuel supply chain, while EPA is directed to fast-track pesticide registrations.

Why it matters: New biofuel demand for feed grains could firm corn and soybean prices — and your feed bill — even as faster crop-protection approvals reshape input choices on the cropping side of the operation.
