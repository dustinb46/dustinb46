Labor & Policy

Washington says dairy can use H-2A — but reads the fine print first

New federal guidance, welcomed by USDA on June 17, clarifies that dairy operations can petition for H-2A guest workers under current law, so long as they document a genuinely temporary or seasonal labor need, judged case by case.

Why it matters: This is the administrative clarification, separate from the year-round H-2A bill floated earlier — and it is narrower than the celebratory headlines suggest. Cornell's workforce specialists note the temporary-need test and existing restrictions still apply, so the practical opening for always-on milking is limited; build petitions around defined seasonal peaks, not year-round staffing.

Manure & Money

California puts $34M on the table for manure upgrades

CDFA's new 2026 Dairy Plus Program is offering $34 million in competitive grants — up to $1.25 million per project — for advanced manure management: solid-liquid separation, weeping walls, vermifiltration, centrifuges and digesters, aimed at both methane and nitrogen/salt surplus. It is funded through USDA's Advancing Markets for Producers initiative; applications close September 14.

Why it matters: Real capital for infrastructure many operations already need. Even outside California, the funded practice list and per-project cost benchmarks are a useful read on which manure technologies regulators are now willing to pay for.

Processing

A $150M cheese-plant rebuild wraps in Wisconsin

Dairy State Cheese completed a $150 million modernization of its Rudolph, Wisconsin plant — eight 55,000-pound vats, new whey separators and protein-concentrate capacity, and a $21 million wastewater upgrade — marked with state officials during June Dairy Month.

Why it matters: It deepens hard-Italian-cheese and whey-protein capacity and locks in a milk home for roughly 120 farms — another sign that processing investment, and the competition for milk, keeps concentrating in cheese and whey rather than fluid.

Compliance

FDA reopens the lot-level traceability fight before the 2028 deadline

FDA held a public meeting on June 15 on "lot-level" traceability under FSMA Rule 204, gathering industry input on possible flexibilities — with enforcement now pushed to July 20, 2028.

Why it matters: Cheeses sit on FDA's Food Traceability List, so processors and anyone holding listed foods face new key-data-element recordkeeping at every handoff. Critics warn the burden lands hardest on smaller operators; the comment window is the moment to shape how lot-level tracking actually works.

Sustainability Economics

Dairy's carbon-credit "gold rush" has stalled

With margins tight, carbon programs are losing momentum: an analysis finds only operations of 2,000+ cows can justify the $28,000–$37,000 upfront cost and 6–12-month payment lags, leaving an estimated 80% of U.S. dairies effectively priced out — even though most already practice at least one sustainability measure.

Why it matters: If you have been weighing a carbon contract, the math is the story: small, slow payments against heavy data entry. Right now, the same practice changes may be worth more as on-farm efficiency than as tradable credits.

Animal Health

New prion study renews cross-species questions on CWD

A University of Calgary–led study published in mid-June found chronic wasting disease prions can spread "silently" — present and infectious in animals showing no symptoms — and can adapt as they move between hosts, potentially seeding new strains.

Why it matters: The barrier to cattle and humans still looks strong, but rising CWD prevalence in wild deer raises surveillance and land-use stakes for livestock operations in endemic regions. Worth tracking as a long-tail biosecurity issue where deer share ground, feed or forage with cattle.

Research & Development

USDA opens $125M to modernize aging ag research labs

USDA and the Department of Education announced a $125-million-a-year Research Facilities Act program, run through NIFA, to rebuild research infrastructure at land-grant universities — competitive grants up to $30 million with a dollar-for-dollar nonfederal match. Applications are due July 17.

Why it matters: The pipeline of applied dairy work — genetics, animal health, feed efficiency — runs through these university facilities. Where this money lands shapes which on-farm problems get solved over the next decade.
