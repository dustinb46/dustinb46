Markets

The Cold Storage print landed neutral — and cheese futures bounced

Thursday's USDA Cold Storage report, the one this brief flagged to watch, came in benign: end-of-May natural cheese stocks sat near 1.41 billion pounds — up about 0.6% from April but roughly 1.5% under a year ago — while butter built seasonally (up about 7% on the month) yet stayed below 2025. Traders read it as neutral and cheese futures rebounded.

Why it matters: The feared cheese-inventory glut didn't show up in the data, which removes a near-term drag on Class III sentiment. If you carry open milk-price hedges, the build-but-below-last-year picture argues against a fast bearish break and buys time before the next catalyst.

Food Safety

A fatal Listeria outbreak just pulled every cheese from one maker

Clover Hill Dairy of Mechanicsville, MD expanded its recall to all cheese products made at its facility after FDA and CDC linked its requesón/ricotta-style soft cheese to a multi-year Listeria outbreak that has killed one person and hospitalized eight. The cheese is frequently relabeled and reaches shelves under names such as Kesso, Quesos La Ricura, Izalco, De Mi Pueblo and Rio Lindo.

Why it matters: A whole-facility recall with private-label spread is exactly how one plant's failure becomes a category-wide buyer-confidence and audit event. If your milk moves into Hispanic-style or soft-cheese channels, expect tighter Listeria environmental scrutiny and documentation demands downstream.

Inputs & Legal

Supreme Court shields glyphosate from failure-to-warn suits

In Monsanto v. Durnell, the Court ruled 7–2 (Kavanaugh writing) that the federal pesticide law FIFRA preempts state failure-to-warn claims, a decision that should clear thousands of pending Roundup cancer-warning lawsuits against Bayer/Monsanto.

Why it matters: Years of litigation had Bayer publicly weighing whether to keep glyphosate on the U.S. market. Lifting that liability overhang makes continued availability and steadier pricing of the cheapest broad-spectrum herbicide more likely — directly relevant to forage and feed-crop weed-control budgets.

Policy

Year-round E15 and $11B in farm aid — riding an Iran-war spending bill

The administration's $87.6 billion supplemental request tied to the war in Iran also asks Congress for $11.1 billion in farm aid ($10B for 2026 row and specialty crops, $1.1B for storm-hit Florida growers), a permanent year-round E15 fix, and a rewrite of hemp law.

Why it matters: Permanent year-round E15 props up corn demand and tends to put a floor under feed-grain prices — a cost headwind for dairy rations. And because the aid is bundled into contentious war spending and aimed at crop farmers, passage is uncertain and dairy isn't a direct beneficiary.

Competitive

The "silicon scramble": AI data centers are bidding up dairy's land and power

A wave of AI data-center development is now competing with farms for land, water and electricity, with dairy-heavy parts of the Midwest seeing farmland push toward record values and around-the-clock data-center load straining rural grids — a tension dairy operators raised directly at this year's industry conferences.

Why it matters: Unlike seasonal farm demand, data centers pull power 24/7, which can lift rural electricity rates and tighten water access — two real on-farm cost lines — while inflated land prices quietly raise the bar on expansion and farm-transfer economics.

Animal Science

A study says cattle were "always" built to digest seaweed

New work in Nature Communications finds that the gut microbes that break down seaweed polysaccharides already lurk at low levels across geographically diverse ruminants and bloom quickly once seaweed enters the diet — a "latent trait." In an eight-cow, 14-day trial, seaweed-fed cows emitted about 60% less methane while staying healthy and productive.

Why it matters: If the methane-cutting response runs through microbes cattle already carry, seaweed-based additives become more biologically plausible at scale — and more likely to anchor the low-methane-milk premiums and credits that buyers are starting to dangle.

Regulatory · EU

Lactalis drags Nutri-Score to the EU's top court

The world's largest dairy company has escalated its fight over the revised Nutri-Score algorithm to the Court of Justice of the EU, arguing the new scoring unfairly penalizes cheese and traditional dairy and improperly classifies some dairy products as drinks.

Why it matters: A CJEU ruling would set how front-of-pack labels score dairy fat right across Europe — shaping how cheese and whole-milk products are positioned against plant-based rivals in a top U.S. export market, and the template other regulators copy.

Outlook

USDA pencils record production costs for 2027 — and it isn't fuel

Fresh USDA projections put 2027 per-acre production costs at record highs for major crops (corn near $952, soybeans ~$701), driven this time not by fuel and fertilizer but by seed, chemicals, repairs, labor, machinery and cash rent.

Why it matters: Higher feed-crop production costs tend to be sticky and feed straight into next year's ration and home-grown-forage math. Farm Bureau's read — that elevated input costs are now "a persistent reality" — is the signal to build into 2027 break-even planning rather than treat as a blip.
