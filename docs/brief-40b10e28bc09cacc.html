01 · Feed

USDA's June 30 acreage and stocks reports lean bearish for the feed bin

USDA pegged 2026 corn plantings at 95.34 million acres (unchanged from March, down 3% from last year) and soybeans at 85.37 million acres (up 5%), while June 1 stocks came in at 5.295 billion bushels of corn — 14% above a year ago — and 1.061 billion bushels of soybeans, up 5%. Traders read the set as neutral for corn and outright bearish for soybeans and meal. Why it matters: ample corn carryover and a growing bean crop point to soft feed and protein-meal costs into the fall feeding season — the single biggest lever under DMC margins and the case for staying light on forward feed coverage.

02 · Milk Price

That powder spike didn't hold: global dairy prices reverse hard

Last edition flagged a decade-high one-day jump in CME nonfat dry milk. That rally has snapped back: at the June 30 GDT Pulse, skim milk powder tumbled 9.9% to about $1.38/lb, butter fell 9.0% to $2.49, and whole milk powder slipped 1.8% to $1.58. The same day CME spot nonfat gave back 6.75¢ to $1.63 on 16 loads, and blocks stalled in the low $1.40s with "plenty of cheese in the countryside." Why it matters: the powder shock proved short-lived, so Class IV and DRP positions layered on during last week's pop are already staring at fast mean-reversion — reassess before the next print.

03 · Food Safety

A soft-cheese Listeria outbreak keeps pulling more brands off shelves

FDA and CDC are investigating a multi-year Listeria monocytogenes outbreak traced to requesón (a ricotta-style soft cheese) made by Clover Hill Dairy of Mechanicsville, MD. On June 18 Clover Hill expanded its recall to all of its cheese products; on June 26 downstream repacker La Ceiba Foods pulled requesón sold under the La Colonia and Selectos Latinos brands across MD, VA and DC — the third brand recalled. Twelve people across four states are ill, 10 hospitalized, one dead. Why it matters: it's a live case study in how one contaminated line propagates through co-packers and private labels — a traceability and buyer-liability warning for anyone selling milk into farmstead or Hispanic-style cheese.

04 · Animal Health

Screwworm containment gets a factory as the pest pushes north

On June 27, U.S. and Mexican officials inaugurated a sterile-fly production plant in Metapa, Chiapas — a joint project costing over $50 million that will ramp to 30 million sterile flies a week by mid-July and eventually up to 100 million, roughly doubling the existing Panama output. New World screwworm has now infected more than 30,000 animals across Mexico and reached the U.S., where the first domestic livestock case since the 1960s was confirmed in a Texas calf in early June. Why it matters: a northward-marching screwworm means expanding quarantine zones, inspection requirements and treatment costs for cattle movement — the sterile-fly ramp is the containment clock every operation moving or buying animals should be watching.

05 · Genetics

Australia sets a hard beef-on-dairy target — a template worth watching

A new industry report, "Beyond the Breed: Unlocking Australia's Beef-on-Dairy Potential," lays out a plan to lift processing to 100,000 beef-on-dairy head a year by 2030, riding wider sexed-semen use, improved beef-on-dairy genetics and Dairy Australia's CalfWays goal of routing 300,000–400,000 non-replacement calves into the beef chain annually by 2035. Why it matters: it's the same crossbred-calf revenue play U.S. dairies are already running, and a signal that the global supply of dairy-beef genetics — and competition for the crossbred calf premium — is set to keep climbing.

06 · Product Strategy

Fonterra drops A2 milk to chase lactose-free

New Zealand cooperative Fonterra is phasing out its Anchor A2 and organic fresh-milk lines for domestic shoppers to concentrate on lactose-free, which it now calls the country's fastest-growing dairy category after protein. Why it matters: a decade of A2 premiumization is cooling at the co-op level; for anyone weighing branded-milk or component positioning, the retail growth signal is pointing at lactose-free and protein — not the A2 story that drove the last cycle.

07 · Competition

Cow-free whey and casein edge toward a U.S. launch

Precision-fermentation dairy proteins keep clearing hurdles: Verley has passed FDA review and raised roughly $38 million to launch fermentation-made whey with U.S. partners by year-end, while Formo secured an FDA "no questions" GRAS letter for its fermented casein. Why it matters: animal-free whey and casein moving toward commercial U.S. shelves is a direct competitive watch item for the high-protein whey and ingredient markets that are currently pulling dairy demand — worth tracking even if the volumes are still small.
