01 · Labor

House moves to crack open H-2A for dairy — but stops short of year-round labor

House Ag Chair G.T. Thompson is formally introducing the Securing Agriculture's Workforce Act (June 30), which redefines "temporary" farm work as any contract under 350 days and moves authority over the definition of "agricultural labor" from the Labor Department to USDA — changes that would let dairies tap the H-2A visa program for the first time, while still stopping short of truly permanent, year-round foreign hires. Why it matters: reliable labor is dairy's tightest operating constraint, and even partial H-2A access plus USDA control of wage and housing rules could reshape labor cost and availability — if the bill survives committee.

02 · Margins

May DMC margin lands at $10.62 — no payout, but feed costs are creeping

USDA's June 29 Agricultural Prices report set the May Dairy Margin Coverage margin at $10.62/cwt (all-milk price up 50¢, total feed cost up 42¢), comfortably above the $9.50 top coverage tier — so no DMC indemnity is triggered for the month. Why it matters: margins remain healthy, but the feed-cost uptick is the variable to track when weighing second-half risk coverage.

03 · Milk Price

Nonfat powder posts its biggest one-day jump in a decade; Class IV rips higher

Ever.Ag's Milk Market Minute reports CME spot nonfat dry milk leapt a dime to $1.6975/lb on June 29 — the largest single-day gain since October 2015 — on a suspected sudden disruption in nonfat production, pulling Q4 Class IV futures up roughly 60¢ to $19.00/cwt. Why it matters: a powder supply shock flows straight into Class IV mailbox prices and changes the math on Class IV and DRP hedges already on the books.

04 · Feed & Policy

USDA finalizes a carbon-scoring rule that ties your corn and soybeans to biofuel credits

The Federal Register published USDA's final Technical Guidelines for Regenerative Agricultural Biofuel Feedstocks on June 29, establishing a field-level carbon-intensity scoring system — with a new USDA FD-CIC calculator — for corn, soybeans, sorghum and spring canola sold into the biofuel supply chain. Why it matters: it opens a premium "low-carbon" market for the exact crops that set dairy feed costs, adding a new pull on grain demand and a fresh variable to feed pricing.

05 · Components

The protein craze is now a milk-routing problem, not just a supplement shortage

With whey protein isolate at record prices and USDA describing product as "largely unavailable" in late June, processors are increasingly steering milk toward high-protein streams over commodity cheese — a structural pull on component values. Why it matters: tight whey markets lift the whey factor in Class III and reward herds shipping high-protein milk, but the real bottleneck is drying and filtration capacity that won't expand meaningfully until 2027.

06 · Environment

A data point for the nutrient debate: water-quality-certified farms out-earn peers for a 7th year

Minnesota Ag Water Quality–certified farms averaged about $197,500 in net farm income in 2025 versus roughly $129,400 for comparable non-certified operations — the seventh straight year of higher profit — while the certification locks in 10 years of regulatory certainty. Why it matters: as dairies face tightening nutrient and drinking-water scrutiny, the data suggests conservation compliance can be margin-positive rather than a pure cost.

07 · Herd Health

A net wrap that survives the baler but not the rumen

A plant-based, certified-compostable bale net wrap (Nature's Net Wrap), tested at the University of Saskatchewan and Lakeland College, breaks down safely if cattle accidentally ingest it, while reportedly saving $2–$5 per bale in removal labor. Why it matters: net-wrap plastic left on bales is a real and under-diagnosed cause of rumen and hardware trouble — a low-cost input swap that removes a herd-health risk.

08 · Regulation

Vermont becomes the first state to ban paraquat — and three neighbors are watching

Vermont's first-in-the-nation ban on the herbicide paraquat (linked to Parkinson's disease) takes effect Nov. 1, with New Jersey, New York and Pennsylvania now debating similar measures. Why it matters: paraquat is a workhorse burndown on corn-silage and forage ground, and a spreading patchwork of state bans would narrow herbicide options and raise weed-control costs for feed growers.
