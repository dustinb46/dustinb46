01 · Animal-Health Radar

Screwworm reaches Texas — and the defense is a year-plus away

USDA confirmed New World screwworm in a calf in Zavala County, Texas (June 3), and the sterile-fly tool that suppresses the parasite is thin: one rearing plant in Panama producing roughly 100 million flies a week, with a new U.S. facility not slated to run until November 2027. Pennsylvania has already issued an animal-movement quarantine (effective June 9). Why it matters: a flesh-eating cattle pest moving north, plus tightening interstate animal-movement rules, is a live biosecurity and herd-health exposure — tighten arrival protocols and watch state orders.

Sources: USDA APHIS · The Spokesman-Review (June 13)
02 · Energy & Tax

The manure-to-RNG credit just got more bankable

The Department of Energy released an updated 45ZCF-GREET model (June 12) used to score the 45Z Clean Fuel Production Credit, and USDA says its feedstock guidance is imminent. Fuel from livestock manure keeps its uncapped, negative-carbon-intensity treatment — so renewable natural gas from a low-CI dairy digester can claim outsized credit value. Why it matters: clearer, more bankable math for anaerobic digesters and manure-to-RNG deals, the financial engine behind a lot of dairy decarbonization offers.

Sources: Biomass Magazine · U.S. Treasury
03 · Federal Lands

USDA moves to reopen grazing on National Forest land

USDA issued a June 12 directive to restore grazing on National Forest System lands and streamline permitting and support for ranchers using federal allotments. Why it matters: for operations that summer heifers or cattle on federal ground, this points to expanded, lower-cost forage access — and signals a friendlier federal posture on grazing permits worth tracking at your local district.

Source: USDA press release (June 12)
04 · Price Desk

June WASDE: more milk, a softer price guide

USDA's June 11 supply-and-demand update raised the 2026 milk-production forecast even as it trimmed the Class III milk price to $16.60/cwt and Class IV to $19.35/cwt, lowering cheese, nonfat dry milk and whey on recent price declines. Why it matters: the federal price outlook that anchors your milk check moved lower while output climbs — margin pressure into the second half, and a reason to revisit risk-management coverage.

Sources: Brownfield Ag News · Ag Proud
05 · Data Desk

NMPF will now publish dairy data twice a year

The National Milk Producers Federation released its first-ever mid-year update to Dairy Data Highlights (June 15), moving the long-running benchmark report from annual to semiannual. Why it matters: fresher national and state figures on herd size, production, trade and consumption — a better mid-season yardstick to size your operation and basis against the rest of the industry.

Source: NMPF
06 · Trade

The USMCA review clock starts July 1 — and dairy has skin in it

U.S. and Mexican negotiators held a second round of agriculture-focused talks on June 16 ahead of the mandatory USMCA joint review that begins July 1, even as the administration publicly questions the pact's future. Why it matters: Mexico and Canada together take roughly 40% of U.S. dairy exports; renewal uncertainty is a direct demand risk for milk powder, cheese and whey, and a swing factor for late-2026 prices.

Sources: High Plains Journal · AS/COA review tracker
★ The Wider Lens — one broad read

Five states now make half the milk

New analysis shows just five states — California, Wisconsin, Idaho, Texas and New York — now produce more than 50% of U.S. milk, with the Lake States and West Coast alone near 45% and the industry's center of gravity sliding toward the High Plains and the I-29 corridor (Kansas output up roughly 24% year over year). Why it matters: scale, processing investment and lower-cost basins are concentrating the industry — the competitive map under every milk check, and a guide to where new plant demand and milk premiums will form next.

Sources: Dairy Herd · Cheese Reporter
